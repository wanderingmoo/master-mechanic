## Why this matters

This is a primary regulation source for the 1968 period. It does not replace Ford, FAV, JWA, Gurney-Weslake, ZF, Girling, or chassis-specific service records, but it sets the period classification and recognition rules that determine which details are configuration-critical.

## Extracted facts

- Appendix J classified Group 4 as recognized-production sportscars.
- The sportscar definition required at least two seats.
- Group 4 sportscars were tied to a 50-car recognition threshold.
- Cylinder-capacity classes included the over-3,000 cc to 5,000 cc class relevant to a 302 cu in / about 4.9 L engine.
- Recognition forms could be required at scrutineering or the start of a competition.
- Group 4 recognition tied engine identity to details including cylinder head, cylinder block, cylinder count, bore, stroke, and crankshaft bearings.
- Group 4 recognition treated ignition, induction, exhaust, carburettor, filters, and manifolds as freer items than the hard engine identity points.
- Group 4 sportscar recognition allowed engine re-boring up to 1.2 mm.
- Gearbox ratio selection was freer than basic gearbox identity; the regulation distinguished recognized gear series from ratio choice.
- Brake-system and suspension principles were regulation-sensitive; these are not safe places to substitute generic later GT40 or replica assumptions.
- Minimum-weight treatment was tied to recognition documents and minimum tables; the table includes 700 kg for the over-3,000 cc to 5,000 cc sportscar class.
- Appendix J also contains safety-equipment provisions for two-seater racing cars, including fire extinguisher, scatter shield where clutch/flywheel failure can endanger the driver, roll bar, safety belt, firewall/floor, tank isolation, enclosed batteries, circuit breaker, and fuel-tank filling/venting requirements. These are indexed as period safety benchmarks, not automatically as Group 4 GT40 requirements.

## Mechanic-facing implications

- The car needs its FIA homologation/recognition form before the assistant can treat a period race specification as authoritative.
- Engine advice must identify the actual block, bore/stroke, heads, and build sheet before applying any Gurney-Weslake 302 setting.
- Induction and ignition freedom under the regulation does not make any carburetor, ignition, or exhaust setting safe; the installed hardware and engine build still control the mechanic-facing setup.
- Driveline advice must identify the gearbox/transaxle hardware before recommending oil, ratios, preload, or shift adjustments.
- Brake and suspension work must preserve as-found evidence before disassembly because component identity affects both legality and safety.
- Period safety provisions are minimum historical context. Current event rules, track rules, and best-practice safety equipment may be stricter and must be checked separately.
